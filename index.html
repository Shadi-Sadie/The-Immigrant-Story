Part One

Who Is an Immigrant?

✦

Nine people. A few questions. No right answers.

I have been an immigrant for most of my adult life.

I know what it feels like to fill out forms that determine whether you belong. To carry documents that expire. To be asked where you are "really from" after you've already answered. To watch people decide what you are before you've said a word.

But here's what I don't know: what's happening on the other side of that moment. What people actually think when they hear the word "immigrant." Whether they are afraid, indifferent, sympathetic — or something more complicated than any of those. Whether their answer changes depending on who the immigrant is.

I built this to find out. Not to prove a point — I genuinely don't know what we'll find. I have assumptions. Some of them are probably wrong.

You'll meet nine people. Answer a few questions about each one. At the end I'll show you what your answers reveal — and how they compare to everyone else who has been through this.

About 12 minutes. All answers are anonymous.

Part Two

The word itself

✦

You just used the word "immigrant" nine times. But where did that word come from, and did it always mean what it means today?

Before we look it up — what do you think the word literally means?

immigrant
From Latin immigrare — "to move into." First appeared in English in the 1780s–1790s.
A person who comes to live permanently in a foreign country.

That definition is only about 230 years old. It appeared during the era when nation-states were forming — when governments first began drawing sharp lines between those who belonged and those who did not.

But people have always moved. Romans settled in Britain. Arab traders migrated across North Africa. Turkic peoples moved into Anatolia. Europeans crossed the Atlantic. None of them were called immigrants. They had other words — words that reveal something different about how their movement was understood.

Tap each label to see what it actually meant — and what it was tracking.

Foreigner
Social · about belonging
Someone from outside the community. The category was social, not administrative — it described how a community felt about someone, not their legal status. It focused on cultural difference, not movement.
Alien
Legal · about loyalty
Used in English law for centuries. Meant someone who owed allegiance to another sovereign — it was about legal loyalty, not the act of moving. Used in laws about property ownership, trade rights, and loyalty to the crown.
Settler
Power · about occupation
Someone establishing residence in new land, often tied to power and expansion. It focused on founding or occupying — and implicitly on the relationship to whoever was already there. A colonial category.
Stranger
Temporal · about familiarity
A temporary outsider. The focus was on time and familiarity, not permanent migration — someone not yet known, who might eventually become known. It described a moment, not a permanent status.

Notice what these four words have in common: none of them describe the act of moving. They describe the relationship to a community — through belonging, loyalty, power, or time.

The word "immigrant" changed that. It made movement itself the defining fact — not why someone came, not how they were received, not whether they belonged. Just the crossing.

Take the same person in two different centuries.

In 1600
A merchant from Florence living in London
a stranger a foreigner a subject of another king
In 1800
The same person, the same journey
an immigrant

The movement didn't change. The person didn't change. What changed was that governments needed a single category to administer people crossing borders — and "immigrant" became that category.

The issue was never the movement. It was always the belonging. And once governments controlled who could belong, they also controlled who the word applied to — and what happened to those people as a result.

That is what the policy history is about. And that is where we are going next.
